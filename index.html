GEOG-349: Portfolio

An Exploration of Raccon Dog Movements
in the Southwest Archipelago of Finland

A cartographic portfolio that looks at movements of invasive raccoon dogs between islands in southwest Finland. The portfolio focuses on the map design choices that were made to create a cohesive and visually compelling narrative.

Introduction and Motivation

The raccoon dog (Nyctereutes procyonoides) is an invasive species that causes major problems for native birds in the Finnish archipelago.

This portfolio is based on a dataset collected by Toivonen, Toivola, and Selonen (2025). They collected data via GPS tracking collars, investigating how the behaviour of raccoon dogs change when ice forms over the sea, as this allows the animals to more easily cross water. I chose this dataset as I recently came across the accompanying paper and am very interested in invasive species, hence I wanted to explore the dataset myself.

Ryzhkov Sergey, CC BY-SA 4.0, via Wikimedia Commons

The Raccoon Dog Movement Dataset consists of time-stamped location records (latitude, longitude, and precision metadata), collected with GPS collars attached on 30 individual raccoon dogs. Additionally, this portfolio uses daily sea-ice concentration grids from the Copernicus Marine Data Store,land boundaries from Finnish Environment Institute (SYKE) and elevation data from National Land Survey of Finland for visualization and environmental context. [See links]

This portfolio explores different visualizations of the dataset, not directly related to the paper itself. In particular, I was interested in exploring ways to clearly visualize the paths as animals tend to move back and forth. This creates overlapping patterns that are difficult to interpret with simple point-to-point lines.

In the lectures we discussed the importance of picking the right projection. This was particularly important in this map, as I wanted to minimize the distortion of the small islands. Picking a metric projection also makes the map easier to read, particularly combined with the grid that I applied.

The type of environment is a challenge in this dataset, as putting names on the hundreds of small islands makes the map cluttered. Instead I have focused on providing other context clues in my maps (in this map the grid, later eg. by enabling zooming).

The concept of Data-Ink Ratio from one of the lectures was particularly on my mind in this aspect. The idea was to use a very simple basemap for the maps to avoid clutter, while still showing the islands as context.

Individual Behavior Maps
Small scale interactive maps to show paths of particular raccoon dog individuals.
Open Fullscreen

The previous maps are very zoomed out, showing trends for the entire dataset. In these maps I explore path visualization more closely, choosing two particular individuals (R3 and R13). The temporal/interactive aspect is particularly important in these maps, as showing the full path at once is very messy.

There are two versions of the map: Least-Cost Path (LCP) and Direct path (toggle above the maps). The LCP algorithm uses elevation data fetched via National Land Survey of Finland to compute the best trajectories with Dijkstras algorithm. The LCP algorithm used is very simple, but the idea was to show the difference that a more "natural" path makes.

I assigned different colors for each individual, which is carried on in the Seasonal Mobility chart as well. This keeps the narrative consistent and makes the Mobility chart feel connected to the maps.

I chose a red color for one of the individuals, as I think it fits the overall color scheme of my portfolio. However, I took particular care to not use red and green in the same visualizations, to make them more color blind friendly.

Conclusions

From the maps we can see that raccoon dogs move fairly locally, not moving across the entire study are but instead staying fairly clustered among a few islands. From the edge bundling map we can see that some islands are visited often, while many are only visited a once or twice.

On an individual scale, the maps show big differences. Some individuals like R3 traverse a lot of different islands, whereas some (like R13) remain largely on one larger island. Interestingly, the Seasonal Mobility chart shows that the individual that move across many islands tends to move longer distances in the winter when the water is covered in ice, whereas R13 moves longer distances in the late summer.

The chart visualizations show that travel distances are longer over ice, which indicates that ice helps the invasive species spread further. This conclusion is also supported by the paper by Toivonen et al. (2025).
